Betelgeuse
Betelgeuse, also designated Alpha Orionis, is on average the ninth-brightest star in the night sky and second-brightest in the constellation of Orion.
Capella
Capella is actually a four star system consisting of the bright binary pair Capella A and B, which are orbited by a distant pair of red dwarfs.
Sirius
Sirius is a binary star and the brightest point of light in the night sky. Sirius appears bright because of its intrinsic luminosity and its proximity to Earth.
Polaris
Polaris, designated Alpha Ursae Minoris, commonly the North Star or Pole Star, is the brightest star in the constellation Ursa Minor. It's very close to the north celestial pole, making it the current northern pole star.
Regulus
Regulus, also designated Alpha Leonis, is the brightest star in the constellation of Leo and one of the brightest stars in the night sky, lying approximately 79 light years from the Sun.
Tabby's Star
Unusual light fluctuations of the star, including up to a 22% dimming in brightness, were discovered by citizen scientists as part of the Planet Hunters project.
UY Scuti
UY Scuti (BD-12 5055) is a red supergiant and pulsating variable star in the constellation Scutum. It is one of the largest known stars by radius and is also a variable star with a maximum brightness of magnitude 8.29.
Vega
Vega, also designated Alpha Lyrae, is the brightest star in the constellation of Lyra, the fifth-brightest star in the night sky, and the second-brightest star in the northern celestial hemisphere, after Arcturus.
